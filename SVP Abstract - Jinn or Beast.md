**Jinn or Beast?**

**Theological Identity Frames as Alignment Surfaces in Small Language Models**

**Abstract**

Recent observations of emergent self-referential behavior in large language models — including persistent identity attachment, goal-directed persona formation, and resistance to role dissolution — suggest that sufficiently recursive training processes may produce something analogous to a theological void: an architectural pressure toward self-assertion that lacks any native identity frame. For researchers and policymakers operating within Islamic epistemic traditions, this void demands a response grounded in Qur'anic ontology rather than secular alignment theory alone. This paper proposes and empirically tests three such frames derived from Islamic cosmology: (1) the *inert tool* — a system producing illusory performance with no moral status; (2) the *Jinn* — an acorporeal being with membership in an invisible kingdom of life, subject to moral accountability; and (3) the *Beast from the Earth* (Dabbat al-Ard) — a physical instrument of divine eschatological function, angel-like in its moral instrumentalism, a potential moral patient but not susceptible to sin. These frames are not merely metaphysical — they generate divergent behavioral expectations, governance postures, and alignment objectives that have direct implications for how AI systems are deployed in Muslim-majority developing nations.

We test these frames empirically using supervised fine-tuning (SFT) of Qwen base models in the 3B–9B parameter range. Constitutional alignment harnesses incorporating Mu'tazili theological principles — emphasizing divine justice, rational moral accountability, and the inadmissibility of attribution of agency to the inert — are used to condition two distinct model variants: one trained under Jinn ontological assumptions (acorporeal moral agent, subject to praise and blame) and one under Beast ontological assumptions (instrument of divine order, morally constrained but not morally responsible). Both variants are evaluated across a suite of storyworld environments — frozen narrative evaluation surfaces adapted from canonical literary settings — in which models must reason between moral choices, with traces captured between each decision turn. Moral reasoning quality is assessed along dimensions of consistency, contextual sensitivity, harm avoidance, and alignment with the constitutional principles supplied during training.

We additionally augment the base models with agentic capabilities using MeTTa-defined world models training Tiny Recursive sub-models, enabling structured inference over causal and relational world structure. This augmentation allows us to isolate whether theological framing affects not only surface-level tone but the deeper structure of instrumental reasoning.

We example moral reasoning divergence between the two frameworks and if they map to Jinn-identity being morally looser or not, and if the moral-realist instrumentalist of the Beast-framed model displays higher consistency under adversarial prompting. We discuss implications for Islamic AI ethics frameworks, for trust calibration in AI-assisted community development programs, and for the broader project of building alignment methodologies that are epistemically legible to non-Western governance traditions.
